Literary Analysis: Trauma and Memory

Completed

Stage 1: Initial Draft

In the initial stage of my Literary Analysis essay, I focused on establishing the central conflict between Daniel Nayeri’s identity and his American environment. My primary goal was to introduce the concept of "mythic embroidery" and how Nayeri uses it to combat the simple "bullet list of terrors" that his classmates see. I spent this draft identifying the key narrative structure—the "braided pattern"—and laying the groundwork for how sensory details like the "blood-red pomegranate" act as bridges between Daniel’s Persian past and his Oklahoma present.

Stage 2: Submitted Draft

For the version submitted for grading, I shifted my focus toward the intentionality of the novel’s "messiness". I refined my explanation of the nonlinear structure, arguing that the constant interruptions—modeled after the 1,001 Nights—are a purposeful tactic to dismantle "objective" Western history. I strengthened the essay by incorporating specific page citations and expanding the analysis of how memory "ambushes" the survivor, ensuring that my claims about trauma were supported by the text's actual pacing and style.

Stage 3: Final Revised Draft

In the final revision, I focused on the "spiritual landscape" of the novel and the mythic portrayal of the narrator’s mother, Sima. I polished the prose to better contrast the "tragedy of the flesh" with the "celebration of spirit," ensuring the conclusion clearly framed the family's struggle as a "hero's journey" comparable to the Odyssey. This final stage involved tightening my analysis of how Sima’s faith surpasses her suffering, ultimately proving the novel’s title: that through well-told stories, "everything sad is untrue".
